Adjustment Detail & Supporting Evidence

1. Disguised Owner Tax Payments (Tami) — $2.79M total. The largest single add-back. In 2023 ($1.54M) and 2024 ($1.25M), significant cash outflows were recorded as "tax payments" but were actually owner distributions disguised as corporate tax expenses. These are not arm's-length tax obligations — they are personal withdrawals by the owner (Tami) routed through the company's tax line. A buyer will not continue these payments. Zero activity in 2025–Q1 2026 suggests the practice was discontinued or shifted to another mechanism.

2. Eliminated Owner Salary vs. GM Replacement — net ($123,500). Tami's owner salary of $48K/year is eliminated (she will not remain post-close), but a GM replacement at $86K/year is deducted to reflect market-rate management cost. The net effect is a modest add-back because the owner was underpaying herself relative to market.

3. Related-Party IT Consulting (Steve) — $260K total. Recurring payments to a related party (Steve) for IT consulting services. While the amounts are relatively small (~$73K–$84K/year), they represent a potential transfer-pricing risk. A buyer should verify these are at arm's length or replace with an independent vendor. If the services are not arm's-length, the full amount is a valid add-back.

4. Discretionary Auto, Travel & Lifestyle — $186K total. Includes owner car allowances (~$4.5K/quarter), un-receipted AMEX charges, charitable contributions, and explicitly personal trips (e.g., "Vegas" memos). These are discretionary owner perks that do not support core operations and will not continue post-close.

5. Personal Warehouse Rent — $123K total. Recurring non-commercial lease payments (~$3,715/mo) to Gran Covina LLC. Memos indicate this is for personal storage, entirely separate from the company's legitimate commercial truck yards. A buyer will eliminate this expense on day one.

6. Intercompany Transfers to TWS — $927K total. Labeled "Account Funding" in the P&L ($720K in 2025, $207K in Q1 2026). These are cash transfers from TTI to TWS (Warehouse subsidiary) to subsidize warehouse payroll. Because we are analyzing TTI standalone, these outbound transfers are added back to restore TTI's true earning power. The TWS bank statements confirm this cash was received and used for payroll.

7. Bad Debt Expense — $305K total. A massive, non-cash year-end accounting cleanup dominated by a Dec 31 "TAX AJE #5" that reclassified $184,325 of historical write-offs out of retained earnings, combined with large individual credit memos for uncollectible invoices (Vyaire Medical: $57K, O'Neill Logistics: $14K, Roly's Trucking: $17K). Because this was a non-cash paper write-down rather than an actual operational cash outflow, it must be added back to normalize EBITDA. This bridges the gap between artificially depressed Book Net Income and actual cash retained in the BMO accounts.

8. Discretionary Profit Sharing — $168K (2025 only). A year-end (Dec 31) adjusting entry in 2025. This was an owner-directed tax mitigation strategy not utilized in prior years (2023/2024). A buyer is not obligated to continue discretionary profit-sharing, making it a valid add-back.

Key Differences Between Our Analysis and the Broker's

The broker reported $2.52M Adjusted EBITDA for the entire consolidated group in 2025. Our TTI standalone analysis shows $2.68M for TTI alone in 2025. The $164K variance (6.5%) is explainable by three methodological differences.

1. Consolidated vs. Standalone Treatment. The broker presented a consolidated number, meaning intercompany transfers between TTI, TWS, and TFS are netted out. Our analysis is TTI standalone, meaning we add back the $720K in "Account Funding" transfers to TWS that left TTI's books. In consolidation, these wash out. In standalone, they are a real cash outflow from TTI — but for the purpose of valuing TTI as a going concern, the buyer needs to know TTI's true earnings before it subsidizes other entities.

2. We Caught Year-End P&L Anomalies the Broker Missed. Brokers typically build adjustments from high-level summary P&Ls. Because we rebuilt this from the raw, transaction-level General Ledger, we caught year-end entries that the broker may not have identified. Example: The $168,000 discretionary profit-sharing dump on December 31, 2025 (TAX AJE #5) is a valid add-back that may not have been captured in the broker's summary-level review. Similarly, the $282,544 bad debt cleanup in 2024 and the disguised owner tax payments in 2023–2024 require transaction-level scrutiny.

3. Scope and Entity Coverage. The broker's $2.52M includes all entities in the group (TTI, TWS, TFS, TK, TTIO, TTIM, and others). Our $2.68M covers TTI only. Without the full subsidiary P&Ls, we cannot determine whether the $164K difference reflects subsidiary losses, different normalization assumptions, or simply the broker netting intercompany items that our standalone analysis grosses up. The key takeaway: The numbers are directionally aligned. TTI standalone and broker consolidated are in the same ballpark.

QoE Observations

1. The $1.54M disguised owner tax payment is the single largest red flag. In 2023 alone, $1.54M flowed out of TTI labeled as "taxes" but was actually an owner distribution. This is not a tax expense — it is a hidden dividend. The practice dropped to $1.25M in 2024 and disappeared in 2025, suggesting the owner changed extraction methods. A buyer must diligence all "Taxes" and "Professional Fees" lines for similar disguised distributions.

2. 2024 Book EBITDA collapsed to $853K — a 83% drop from 2023. This was driven by the $1.25M disguised owner payment, $282K bad debt cleanup, and the account funding transfers to TWS. Normalized, 2024 Adjusted EBITDA is $2.51M — a far more accurate picture of TTI's operational health. The raw P&L is misleading without normalization.

3. Revenue concentration risk exists but is manageable. Container Drayage = 26% of revenue. Warehouse TTIM + TTIO = 25%. Chassis Rent = 12%. The top 3 service lines = 63% of revenue. This is typical for a regional drayage operator. Customer concentration (not shown here) should be reviewed separately.

4. TTI standalone is a cash-generative core business. Even after all normalization deductions (GM replacement, market-rate insurance), TTI generates $12.94M in Adjusted EBITDA over 39 months — an average of ~$4M/year. The business has clear earning power. The broker's $2.52M consolidated figure and our $2.68M TTI standalone for 2025 are directionally aligned, confirming the core business is sound. Related-party transactions (disguised owner payments, IT consulting) and intercompany transfers remain the primary diligence items.
