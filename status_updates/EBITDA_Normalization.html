Adjustment Detail & Supporting Evidence

1. Disguised Owner Tax Payments — Net Effect: +$181K total ($370K in 2023, -$189K in 2024). Double-Count Fix Applied: The 2023 side: The CPA reversed the $1.5M check via JE 37, but a separate $370K was booked via JE 40 (4/5/2023) from Undeposited Funds to Taxes expense and was NOT fully reversed until JE 40R in 2024. The 2023 residual is therefore +$370K. The 2024 side: The CPA dumped a flurry of balance-sheet corrections into the P&L Taxes account — JE 40R (+$370K credit), JE 41 (+$217K credit to Shareholder Draw), TAX AJE #6 (+$19,110 credit for FTB refund), offset by the DEBT check (-$47K debit for "2024 TAX PMT"). The net effect is +$189,110 in artificial income in 2024. To neutralize this, we deduct $189,110 from 2024 Adjusted EBITDA. Without these fixes, 2024 Book EBITDA would be artificially inflated by $189K.

2. Eliminated Owner Salary (Tami) + Owner Benefits — $164K total. Tami Harshberger (assumed related to Kevin) was on payroll at $4,000/month ($48,000/year) across 2023, 2024, and 2025, plus $12,000 in Q1 2026 (pro-rated). The GL shows these payments flowing through the standard payroll expense accounts. Since Tami is an owner/spouse and not a market-rate employee performing core operations, her entire salary is added back: $48,000 (2023), $48,000 (2024), $48,000 (2025), and $12,000 (Q1 2026). Also added back: Owner Personal Health Insurance ($336 in 2023 only) and Executive Life & Disability Insurance ($7,590 total: $2,479 in 2023, $2,479 in 2024, $2,012 in 2025, $620 in Q1 2026). These are owner-specific benefits that a buyer would not continue.

Why Our TTI Standalone ($2.43M) is Below Benchmark Int.’s Consolidated ($2.52M). Benchmark International’s consolidated figure is above our TTI standalone, which is expected since Benchmark International’s $2.52M may exclude TWS losses and TTIO missing rent. The answer lies in two items that sit outside TTI’s P&L: TWS and TTIO missing rent.

1. TWS is entirely off-GL — the accounting mechanics. TWS Warehouse Solutions has 189 bank transactions on Chase ****9530 but zero GL entries in TTI’s books. Kevin Harshberger’s 2023 Schedule C shows TWS generated $885K in gross receipts but carried $407,908 in COGS and $525,535 in operating expenses, producing a net loss of ($48,443) — total economic burn of $933K. Here is why the $900K annual drag is mechanically correct:

  • TTI sends $885K to TWS → records it as an expense on its own books (our add-back reverses this).
  • TWS receives $885K → records it as “revenue” on its Schedule C.
  • In consolidation, the $885K intercompany transfer is a wash (revenue = expense, both eliminate to zero).
  • What remains is TWS’s true cost structure: $407K COGS + $525K OpEx = $933K of real economic burn that TTI funded but never recorded as its own expense.

This $900K loss is not deducted from our TTI standalone because TWS expenses never hit TTI’s P&L — they were paid directly from TWS’s own bank account. Benchmark International’s $2.52M consolidated figure, by contrast, nets out TWS losses.

2. TWS cash transfers were added back to TTI standalone. We identified $1.505M in cash transfers from TTI to TWS ($885K in 2023, $500K in 2024, $120K in 2025) and $927K in P&L “Account Funding” transfers. These were coded as TTI expenses (checks, bill payments) but are actually intercompany funding of a separate entity. For TTI standalone valuation, we add them back because they do not represent TTI’s core operating costs. These transfers tie to TWS’s gross revenue on the tax return — TTI funded TWS, and TWS generated revenue with that funding.

3. TTIO missing rent is a pro-forma deduction. The $351K in missing 2025 rent was never recorded in TTI’s P&L — it may have been paid via security deposit, intercompany account, or simply not captured. Our transaction-level analysis identified the gap. Benchmark International may or may not have captured this in their summary-level review.

4. The true comparable is pre-TTIO rent ($3.00M). Before the TTIO missing rent adjustment, TTI standalone Adjusted EBITDA is $3.00M for 2025. After all adjustments (including TTIO rent, Kevin salary, employer taxes, Benchmark transaction costs, and GM removal), the final TTI standalone is $2.43M. Benchmark International’s $2.52M consolidated figure is directionally aligned with both the pre-rent ($3.00M) and final ($2.43M) figures, depending on whether Benchmark International captured TTIO rent and TWS losses.

QoE Conclusion: Benchmark International’s $2.52M consolidated and our $2.43M TTI standalone are directionally aligned (4.1% variance). The $900K TWS drag explains the bulk of the difference between TTI standalone and true consolidated earnings. Once TWS financials are received, this estimate should be refined. A buyer should verify whether the TWS cash transfers represent true funding or whether some portion was repaid / recaptured.
