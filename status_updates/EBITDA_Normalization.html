Adjustment Detail & Supporting Evidence

1. Disguised Owner Tax Payments — Net Effect: +$181K total ($370K in 2023, -$189K in 2024). Double-Count Fix Applied: The 2023 side: The CPA reversed the $1.5M check via JE 37, but a separate $370K was booked via JE 40 (4/5/2023) from Undeposited Funds to Taxes expense and was NOT fully reversed until JE 40R in 2024. The 2023 residual is therefore +$370K. The 2024 side: The CPA dumped a flurry of balance-sheet corrections into the P&L Taxes account — JE 40R (+$370K credit), JE 41 (+$217K credit to Shareholder Draw), TAX AJE #6 (+$19,110 credit for FTB refund), offset by the DEBT check (-$47K debit for "2024 TAX PMT"). The net effect is +$189,110 in artificial income in 2024. To neutralize this, we deduct $189,110 from 2024 Adjusted EBITDA. Without these fixes, 2024 Book EBITDA would be artificially inflated by $189K.

3. Related-Party IT Consulting (Steve) — $260K total. Recurring payments to a related party (Steve) for IT consulting services. While the amounts are relatively small (~$73K–$84K/year), they represent a potential transfer-pricing risk. A buyer should verify these are at arm's length or replace with an independent vendor. If the services are not arm's-length, the full amount is a valid add-back.

4. Discretionary Auto, Travel & Lifestyle — $186K total. Includes owner car allowances (~$4.5K/quarter), un-receipted AMEX charges, charitable contributions, and explicitly personal trips (e.g., "Vegas" memos). These are discretionary owner perks that do not support core operations and will not continue post-close.

5. Personal Warehouse Rent — $123K total. Recurring non-commercial lease payments (~$3,715/mo) to Gran Covina LLC. Memos indicate this is for personal storage, entirely separate from the company's legitimate commercial truck yards. A buyer will eliminate this expense on day one.

6. Intercompany Transfers to TWS — $927K total. Labeled "Account Funding" in the P&L ($720K in 2025, $207K in Q1 2026). These are cash transfers from TTI to TWS (Warehouse subsidiary) to subsidize warehouse payroll. Because we are analyzing TTI standalone, these outbound transfers are added back to restore TTI's true earning power. The TWS bank statements confirm this cash was received and used for payroll.

7. TWS Cash Transfers — $1.505M total ($885K in 2023, $500K in 2024, $120K in 2025). Actual cash transfers from TTI bank accounts to TWS bank accounts, separate from the P&L "Account Funding" line. These represent direct cash subsidies to the warehouse subsidiary that reduce TTI's standalone earnings. A buyer acquiring TTI alone would not continue funding TWS operations. The cash transfer amounts are derived from bank statement analysis and are additive to the P&L-based intercompany transfers.

8. REG Logistics Security Deposit — $100K (2023 only). A $100,000 payment to REG Logistics (the third-party operator managing the TTIO warehouse) was miscategorized as an operating expense in 2023. This was a refundable security deposit, not a true P&L cost. A buyer would recover this deposit at closing or it would transfer as a prepaid asset. Restoring it to EBITDA corrects the miscategorization and reflects TTI's true 2023 earning power.

9. TTIM Security Deposit — $120K (2025 only). On March 11, 2025, a $359,663 check to TTIM included February/March rent plus a $120,000 security deposit. The bookkeeper dumped the entire amount into the TTIM expense account. Security deposits belong on the Balance Sheet, not the P&L. This $120,000 add-back corrects the miscategorization and restores 2025 EBITDA to its true level.

10. Bad Debt Expense — $305K total. A massive, non-cash year-end accounting cleanup dominated by a Dec 31 "TAX AJE #5" that reclassified $184,325 of historical write-offs out of retained earnings, combined with large individual credit memos for uncollectible invoices (Vyaire Medical: $57K, O'Neill Logistics: $14K, Roly's Trucking: $17K). Because this was a non-cash paper write-down rather than an actual operational cash outflow, it must be added back to normalize EBITDA. This bridges the gap between artificially depressed Book Net Income and actual cash retained in the BMO accounts.

11. Discretionary Profit Sharing — $168K (2025 only). A year-end (Dec 31) adjusting entry in 2025. This was an owner-directed tax mitigation strategy not utilized in prior years (2023/2024). A buyer is not obligated to continue discretionary profit-sharing, making it a valid add-back.

12. Pro-Forma Rent Adjustment (Pacific Expressway, Inc. — TTIM) — ($120K) unfavorable to 2024. September 2024 rent was paid directly from a security deposit, bypassing the P&L entirely. The cash went straight to the landlord, so the P&L shows no rent expense for that month. This is a true economic cost that a buyer will incur post-close. Adding it back to 2024 expenses reduces Adjusted EBITDA by $120,000 to reflect the normalized rent obligation.

13. Missing January 2026 Base Rent (Prologis — TTIM) — ($61,889) unfavorable to Q1 2026. The January 2026 check to the landlord was labeled "JAN 2026 TAX & OC" (operating costs / NNN fees) for only $19,390. The base rent itself was never recorded — February's check was ~$61,889, confirming January should have been similar. Because no base rent hit the P&L in January, Q1 2026 is missing a true economic cost. This pro-forma deduction corrects the understatement.

14. Freight Expense Timing / Period Cutoff — net $116,932. These adjustments apply to freight costs only. Because we stop the clock on March 31, 2026, Q1 2026's Book EBITDA is artificially understated by $115,773 on the freight line. In early January 2026, the P&L absorbed $115,773 of freight expenses that actually belonged to 2025 (pushed forward by the CPA's year-end cutoff). While 2024 and 2025 largely net out (-$12,145 vs. +$13,304), Q1 2026 does not get the benefit of pushing its own late-March bills into April. This is a mechanical timing adjustment, not a normalization of operations. Without this fix, Q1 2026 Adjusted EBITDA would understate true earnings by $115,773.

15. Normalized Bad Debt (Per NWC Reserve) — ($339,050) total. The NWC reserve analysis identified normalized bad debt of $59,661 (2023), $88,489 (2024), and $190,900 (2025) based on actual reserve requirements rather than the prior AR aging waterfall estimate. The primary exposure remains BECTON DICKINSON CASS ($78K) and other dormant accounts with 12+ months of non-payment. Unlike the Bad Debt Expense add-back (which was a non-cash accounting cleanup), this adjustment reflects actual uncollectable receivables that a buyer will never realize. It is a bona fide deduction from Adjusted EBITDA. For 2025 specifically, the raw Adjusted EBITDA of $2.75M drops to $2.56M after normalization — now only $36K above the broker's $2.52M consolidated figure.

16. TTIO REG Logistics — Missing 3 Months Rent (2025) — ($351,000) unfavorable to 2025. The TTIO facility (10299 Sixth St., Rancho Cucamonga) was under lease to REG Logistics at $117,000/month through April 30, 2026. Our transaction-level review identified that 3 months of base rent in 2025 were never recorded in the P&L — the GL shows no rent expense for these periods despite the lease obligation continuing. This is a true economic cost: a buyer inheriting the lease (or the successor lease at 8700 White Oak Ave.) will face this rent obligation. At $117K/month × 3 months = $351,000. This pro-forma deduction corrects the 2025 understatement.

17. TTIO REG Logistics — Missing 2 Months Rent (Q1 2026) — ($234,000) unfavorable to Q1 2026. Continuing the TTIO lease analysis, 2 additional months of base rent in Q1 2026 (January and February 2026) were also not recorded in the P&L. At $117K/month × 2 months = $234,000. This further reduces Q1 2026 Adjusted EBITDA. Combined with the 2025 missing rent, the total TTIO rent gap is $585,000 across the 5 months. The landlord may have applied a security deposit or the rent may have been paid through an intercompany account not captured in TTI's P&L — this requires confirmation.

18. Kevin Harshberger — Owner Salary & Holiday Pay — +$371,800 total. Kevin Harshberger consistently drew exactly $114,400 per year ($112,200 salary + $2,200 holiday pay) across 2022, 2023, 2024, and 2025. For QoE purposes, this entire amount is added back to Adjusted EBITDA for every historical year: $114,400 (2023), $114,400 (2024), $114,400 (2025), and $28,600 (Q1 2026, pro-rated). This is a standard EBITDA normalization: owner compensation is treated as a discretionary draw, not a true operating cost. Michael J. Johnson is the actual GM already on payroll at $161K/year (2025), so no replacement cost is needed — the business already has market-rate management. Kevin's $114K draw is purely an owner distribution. This adjustment brings our TTI standalone to $2.61M for 2025 (including employer payroll taxes).

19. Employer Payroll Taxes (Kevin & Tami) — +$42,250 total. The company paid the employer portion of FICA, Medicare, and state unemployment taxes on Kevin Harshberger and Tami's wages. For Kevin, employer taxes cost roughly $9,000 annually. For Tami, roughly $4,000 annually. Combined: $13,000/year. Since both Kevin's and Tami's salaries are added back to EBITDA, the associated employer payroll taxes must also be added back — they are directly tied to owner compensation that will not continue post-close. Michael J. Johnson's employer taxes remain in EBITDA because he is the actual GM staying with the business. This adjustment applies to all historical years: $13,000 (2023), $13,000 (2024), $13,000 (2025), and $3,250 (Q1 2026, pro-rated).

Key Differences Between Our Analysis and the Broker's

The broker reported $2.52M Adjusted EBITDA for the entire consolidated group in 2025. Our TTI standalone analysis shows $2.75M pre-rent-adjustment, $2.56M after bad debt normalization, $2.40M after the $351K TTIO missing rent adjustment, and $2.61M after Kevin Harshberger's owner salary add-back, removal of GM replacement, and employer payroll taxes. The numbers are directionally aligned (3.5% above broker).

1. Consolidated vs. Standalone Treatment. The broker presented a consolidated number, meaning intercompany transfers between TTI, TWS, and TFS are netted out. Our analysis is TTI standalone, meaning we add back both the $927K in P&L "Account Funding" transfers to TWS and the $1.5M in actual cash transfers from TTI to TWS ($885K in 2023, $500K in 2024, $120K in 2025). In consolidation, these wash out. In standalone, they are real cash outflows from TTI — but for the purpose of valuing TTI as a going concern, the buyer needs to know TTI's true earnings before it subsidizes other entities.

2. We Caught Year-End P&L Anomalies the Broker Missed. Brokers typically build adjustments from high-level summary P&Ls. Because we rebuilt this from the raw, transaction-level General Ledger, we caught year-end entries that the broker may not have identified. Example: The $168,000 discretionary profit-sharing dump on December 31, 2025 (TAX AJE #5) is a valid add-back that may not have been captured in the broker's summary-level review. Similarly, the $282,544 bad debt cleanup in 2024 and the disguised owner tax payments in 2023–2024 require transaction-level scrutiny.

3. Scope and Entity Coverage. The broker's $2.52M includes all entities in the group (TTI, TWS, TFS, TK, TTIO, TTIM, and others). Our $2.61M covers TTI only (after all adjustments). The broker's figure is now below our TTI standalone, with the $89K delta attributable to: (a) Kevin Harshberger's owner salary add-back ($114K), removal of GM replacement ($86K), and employer payroll taxes ($13K) partially offset by the TTIO missing rent ($351K), (b) TWS/other entity contributions, or (c) different normalization assumptions. The key takeaway: The TTIO missing rent remains a material diligence item, but the Kevin salary add-back, employer payroll taxes, and Michael Johnson's confirmed GM status restore directional alignment with the broker.

QoE Observations

1. The disguised owner tax payments are a material red flag — but the CPA already fixed most of it. In 2023, a $1.54M check was recorded as "taxes" but was actually an owner distribution. However, the CPA reversed $1.50M of this via year-end JE 37, leaving only $40K as a true economic add-back. Similarly, in 2024, a $1.20M check was reversed via TAX AJE #1, leaving only $47K. The net un-reversed amount is $87K — not $2.79M. This is still a red flag for internal controls (the owner was drawing cash disguised as tax payments), but the P&L impact is far smaller than it first appeared.

2. 2024 Book EBITDA collapsed to $853K — a 83% drop from 2023. This was driven by the disguised owner payment (net $47K after CPA reversal), $282K bad debt cleanup, and the account funding transfers to TWS. Normalized, 2024 Adjusted EBITDA is $1.68M — a far more accurate picture of TTI's operational health. The raw P&L is misleading without normalization. The CPA's year-end reversals artificially depressed 2024 Book EBITDA, making the normalization essential.

3. Revenue concentration risk exists but is manageable. Container Drayage = 26% of revenue. Warehouse TTIM + TTIO = 25%. Chassis Rent = 12%. The top 3 service lines = 63% of revenue. This is typical for a regional drayage operator. Customer concentration (not shown here) should be reviewed separately.

4. TTI standalone is a cash-generative core business. Even after all normalization deductions, the double-count fix (CPA reversals), the timing adjustment, the bad debt normalization, and the TTIO missing rent, TTI generates $10.77M in Adjusted EBITDA over 39 months — an average of ~$3.3M/year. The business has clear earning power. The broker's $2.52M consolidated figure and our $2.61M TTI standalone for 2025 are directionally aligned (3.5% above broker). Kevin Harshberger's $114K annual owner salary add-back, employer payroll taxes ($13K/year), and Michael Johnson's confirmed GM status ($161K/year) were the key adjustments. The $370K disguised owner tax payment (2023) was the largest single add-back, offset by the $189K artificial income correction in 2024.

5. Q1 2026 has two offsetting adjustments. The freight timing adjustment adds $115,773 (freight expenses pushed from 2025 into Jan 2026). However, January 2026 base rent of $61,889 never hit the P&L — only operating costs were recorded. After applying both adjustments, Q1 2026 Adjusted EBITDA is $1.14M — a more accurate reflection of the quarter's true earnings power.

6. T-K Investments is a break-even pass-through — but it hides $243K in personal expenses. The $3.86M intercompany loop (TTI Subcontracted Services ↔ T-K Revenue) washes to zero in consolidation. T-K's operating expenses replace the opaque Subcontracted Services line dollar-for-dollar. The only value in peeling back T-K is the normalization add-backs: $208K Anaheim ghost lease (personal rent with no corporate facility mapping), $28K entity taxes (standard EBITDA add-back), and $7.5K Classic Import (personal auto repairs). TTI + T-K consolidated Adjusted EBITDA is $11.01M (39 months) vs. TTI standalone $10.77M. The broker's $2.52M consolidated figure is now below our TTI + T-K 2025 figure of $2.68M.
