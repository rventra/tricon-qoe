Methodology — Cash-Free, Debt-Free Basis

M&A Rule Applied: "Cash-Free, Debt-Free." All cash (~$4.4M), Line of Credit debt, and vehicle loans (including a 2020 Porsche Macan) have been stripped from the balance sheet. The peg reflects only the operating liquidity required to run the business day-to-day.

Why cash is excluded: Under standard M&A practice, the seller retains all cash on the balance sheet at closing. The buyer does not purchase the seller's cash — the purchase price is for the operating business only. The NWC peg therefore excludes cash accounts entirely and focuses solely on operating current assets (receivables, prepaids) and operating current liabilities (payables, accrued expenses). This ensures the buyer funds only the working capital needed to run the business, not the seller's personal liquidity.

Adjustment 1 — $19,930 Flat Reclassification: A flat $19,930 monthly adjustment is applied to both Current Assets and Current Liabilities. This corrects ancient negative A/R (pre-2017 dormant credit balances) by reclassifying them to Accounts Payable. Because the adjustment hits both sides equally, it has zero net impact on NWC — it simply puts the balances in the right buckets.

Adjustment 2 — $374,543 Bad Debt Waterfall: Historical A/R is adjusted using a "waterfall" method that removes $374,543 in uncollectable receivables chronologically based on the exact month the bad invoices were generated. This is a real reduction to NWC because these receivables will never convert to cash.

Final Calculation: The Trailing Twelve Month (TTM) average of the Adjusted Operating NWC is computed across the 24-month observation period (Jan 2024 – Dec 2025). The locked peg of $1,984,123 represents the normalized operating working capital the buyer should expect to fund at closing.

QoE Observations

1. The NWC peg is well-supported by 24 months of data. With two full years of monthly balance sheet snapshots, the $1,984,123 peg has a solid empirical foundation. Seasonality is visible — Q3 tends to be the peak NWC period (Jul–Sep) when A/R swells before year-end collections.

2. The $374,543 bad debt waterfall is the largest adjustment. This represents receivables that are >90 days old and effectively uncollectable. The waterfall method allocates the write-off to the exact months the invoices were generated, giving a more accurate historical NWC picture than a blunt year-end write-off.

3. The $19,930 reclassification is a mechanical fix. Pre-2017 credit balances were sitting in A/R as negative amounts. Reclassifying them to A/P does not change total NWC — it merely puts the liability in the right bucket. This adjustment is consistent every month.

4. Seasonal swing is material. Monthly Adjusted NWC ranges from a low of $1,244,743 (May 2024) to a high of $2,515,005 (Aug 2025) — a $1.27M swing. Buyers should be aware that Q3 working capital needs can be 2× the Q2 trough. The TTM peg smooths this seasonality.

5. Related-Party Transaction: $99,682 Loan Receivable to TK. A $99,682 "Loan Receivable" to TK (the sister company) was discovered on the balance sheet and stripped from Working Capital as a non-operating item. This flags the need to search the P&L for disguised management fees, shared rent allocations, or other intercompany charges that may not be at arm's length. Any such items would be normalization adjustments to EBITDA.
