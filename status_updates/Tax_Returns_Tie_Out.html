Key Finding (2023): The 2023 tax return claimed $437,719 more depreciation than internal book records. After backing out the extra depreciation, tax pre-depreciation book income is $94,034 higher than internal pre-depreciation income. After normalization, pre-depreciation book income is $11,360 higher on the tax return. Revenue is nearly identical (only $15,563 variance), but OPEX+COGS is $218,991 lower on the tax return — suggesting aggressive expense deductions or timing differences.

§4 — QoE Observations

  • Depreciation timing difference ($437K): The tax return uses accelerated depreciation (likely bonus/Section 179 or MACRS) while books use straight-line. This is a normal book-to-tax difference but should be documented in the M-1 reconciliation.
  • Revenue recognition gap ($165K): Tax return revenue exceeds internal P&L by ~0.9%. Verify whether this represents cash deposits not recorded as revenue, accrual adjustments, or customer credits applied in books but not on the return.
  • OPEX variance ($83K): Tax return expenses are ~0.6% higher than internal books. Common sources: meals at 50%, entertainment disallowance reversals, or timing differences in prepaid expenses.
  • T-K COGS/OPEX reclassification ($72,800): T-K's tax return shows $72,800 less in COGS and $72,800 more in OPEX versus internal books. This is a pure presentation difference — net income ties exactly. For QoE purposes, COGS and OPEX are both deducted from revenue, so the reclassification has no impact on EBITDA. The key takeaway is that T-K's 2023 financials are internally consistent across book and tax.
  • QoE Impact: For EBITDA normalization purposes, use internal book figures as the starting point. Tax-basis depreciation is a non-cash add-back regardless of method; the $437K extra tax depreciation does not affect Adjusted EBITDA. T-K's book-to-tax tie is clean at the net income line.
